Discovery of Canada

The Palaeoindians

The first humans to arrive in Canada were prehistoric humans who arrived
in northwestern Yukon over 12 000 years ago. They crossed a land bridge which
connected Siberia with Alaska and modern day Canada. Eventually, they spread
all across North America. These people were known as the Palaeoindians. They
were considered Indiginous peoples. Back then, Canada was still covered in
huge ice sheets. The palaeoindians ate and hunted caribou, and muskoxen among
other animals. They also fished, and hunted small game to support themselves
By 10 000 years ago, they habited most of Canada. The Indiginous people created
their own society and customs. Indiginous art was discovered in various places
dating back to around 2000-3000 years ago. Around the same time, evidence of
farming was also discovered.

European Arrival

The discovery of Canada by the europeans started in the 16th
century. A French explorer named Jacques Cartier travelled across the Atlantic Ocean
in search for new land for the King. He landed near what is now Gaspe, Quebec.
Cartier was excited, but failed to create a permanent settlement. In 1603, a
person named Samuel de Champlain founded a colony in Canada known as New France
that resided along the St. Lawrence river. He created many notable cities that
still exist today, including Quebec City and Port-Royal. Samuel de Champlain was
one of the most important people in shaping Canada. The government of France created
the establishment of Montreal which is still one of the most important cities in
Canada today. The British landed in North America near the same time as Champlain
did. They created settlements along the east coast of what is now the US. The
British and the French were both very competitive and determined to claim new land.
One of the most significant economical industries was the Fur Trade. Animals
(especially beavers) were killed, and their pelts were sent back to Europe to be
made into clothing. There was a lot of competition between the nations as to who had
more control over the industry. By the 18th century, tensions were really high. The
Seven Years War started in 1756 as a result of worsenting relations between France
and England. The war was won by England, and most of France's territory was given to
England. However, the England turned New France into a British Colony named
Quebec, which operated under the Quebec Act, which preserved French Culture.
